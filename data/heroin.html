Heroin and Opioid-related deaths on Long Island

Nassau and Suffolk County lead New York State in opioid-related deaths, amidst a nation-wide epidemic.

Learn more »

Long Island continues to face an increase in the number of opiate prescription and heroin overdoses, with Suffolk and Kings county leading the state in opioid-related deaths in 2014.
The heroin epidemic on Long Island is part of a nation-wide drug issue that has grown dramatically, with national heroin overdose deaths increasing 200% from 2010 to 2014, according to data from the National Institute of Drug Abuse.
The reasons for the increase of opioid prescription and heroin related deaths vary.
"My cousin was in an automobile accident, where the drugs they gave him for pain affected him," shared a source who requested to remain anonymous. "Then when he couldn't get them he fell into a bad place with heroin."
According to the American Society of Addiction Medicine, the majority of new heroin users begin by misusing prescription painkillers. In 2012, 259 million prescription were written for opioids, flooding the market with addictive painkillers.

Once hooked on prescription opioids, addicts find a more economical option on the streets: heroin.
"Pills can range from $30 to $40 each, but heroin gets as cheap as $10 for a dose," the anonymous source said.
New York has begun implementing legislation and programs to try to help combat the rampant heroin problem.
The Department of Health began a partnership in January with CVS Pharmacy to allow an overdose-reversing drug available without prescription. The drug, Narcan, "counteracts the depression of the central nervous system and respiratory system, allowing an overdose victim to breathe normally," according to New York State Health Commissioner, Dr. Howard Zucker.
According to the CDC, Narcan has reversed over 26,000 overdoses.
"[An ordinary citizen] can also take a naloxone training to learn how to reverse an opioid overdose in case they witness one," said Susan A. Craig, Director of the NYS Office of Alcoholism and Substance Abuse Services (OASAS).
OASAS provides a number of other services to help those struggling with addiction, such as web services to help access treatment and educate family members.
